\subsection{Strange Modules}\label{strange-modules}


\subsection{Questions (and some answers)}
\begin{enumerate}
\item Is dualization an anti-equivalence of finitely adequate modules?

      Yes.
\item Are finitely adequate modules closed under extension?

      If the quotient module is also finitely generated, then yes.
\item\label{ad-hom-closed} Are finitely adequate modules closed under $\Hom$?

      Yes, \Cref{prp:fin-adeq-hom-closure}.
\item Are finitely adequate modules closed under $\_\otimes\_$?

      Yes, for finite free \(B\), by \Cref{ad-hom-closed} and \(A\otimes B^\ast=\Hom(B,A)\), but no in general:


\item Does exponentiating with closed propositions preserve finitely adequate?

      Yes, by partial answer to \Cref{finite-exp-closed}.
\item\label{finite-exp-closed} Are projective/proper/finite-scheme products of finitely adequate modules finitely adequate?

      Partial answer:
      \begin{enumerate}[(i)]
      \item Let $X$ be a finite scheme, i.e. $X$ an affine scheme such that $R^X$ is a finitely presented $R$-module.
      For any finitely adequate-module $M:X\to\Mod{R}_{\mathrm{ad}}$ on $X$, $(x:X)\to M_x$ is finitely adequate: We can exponentiate the diagram witnessing that it is an finitely adequate-module and we can globalize the presentations of pointwise fp modules.
      Exponentiating is left exact and since $M$ is wqc and $X$ affine, it is also right exact.
      \end{enumerate}

\item Are the cohomology groups of finitely adequate module bundles on projective schemes finitely adequate?

      None so far. It seems a bit much to ask, but it would be really great for Serre-Duality, so it should be worth it to try to adapt e.g.\ \cite[19.1.3]{vakil}.

\item Is any finitely adequate-module quasi-coherent?

      No. The kernel $K$ of $2\cdot\_:R\to R$ can not be (strongly) quasi-coherent in general.
      So far there is only an external argument: \url{https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mdjq91Gc6Lo} around 1:10:00 states that strongly quasi-coherent modules are invariant under pullback. But in general $K$ can change between trivial and non-trivial on different stages.
\item Does being finitely adequate depend only on the underlying type?  Maybe related to being an fppf sheaf?
\item What are examples of modules that are not finitely adequate?

      $R^\mathbb{N}$.  A direct sum of countable copies of $R$.  (Citations/proofs?)

      Are there some that are ``smaller'' in some sense?
      What about the ones mentioned in \Cref{strange-modules}?


\item What are examples of modules that are not infinitesimally linear/tangential?

      $\mathrm{Nil}(R)$ is infinitesimally linear but not tangential.

      Denote by $R[S]$ the free $R$-module generated by a set $S$.
      Let $M :\equiv R[\D(2)]/R[\D(1)\vee\D(1)]$ and say $\eta : \D(2) \to R[\D(2)]$ is the constructor map, which induces a pointed map $\tilde{\eta} : \D(2) \to M$.
      By construction we have $\tilde{\eta}|_{\{0\} \times \D(1)} = \tilde{\eta}|_{\D(1) \times \{0\}} = 0$ but since $\D(1)\vee\D(1) \lhook\joinrel\to \D(2)$ is provably not surjective, we have $\tilde{\eta} \neq 0$.
      Hence $M$ is not infinitesimally linear (and in particular not finitely adequate).

      If $R$ has characteristic $p$, then $\hood{R,0}{p-1}$ is not tangential. Its tangent space is $R$ by the same above reasoning.

\item What is the relation of finite adequacy and finitely presentedness/linear adequacy?

      \begin{lemma}
        Not every finitely adequate $R$-module is either finitely presented or linearly adequate.
        Specifically, this is not the case for the family of ideals $(r)$, for $r:R$.
      \end{lemma}

      \begin{proof}
        We first show that if $(r)$ is linearly adequate, then $(r=0) \lor (r \neq 0)$. Indeed, linearly adequate modules are injective in the category of finitely adequate $R$-modules, so we get a lift
        \[
        \begin{tikzcd}
        & R \ar[d, "\cdot r", twoheadrightarrow] \\
        (r) \ar[r, "id"]\ar[ur, "\exists s", dashed] & (r)
        \end{tikzcd}
        \]
        meaning that $(r)$ is a direct summand of $R$ such that $(r) = (e)$ for an idempotent $e:R$.
        Since $R$ is local, $(e=0) \lor (e=1)$ and thus $(r=0) \lor (r \neq 0)$.

        A dual argument shows that if (r) is finitely presented, then also $(r=0) \lor (r \neq 0)$.
        But assuming that all $(r)$ were to admit either one of these presentations, we would get $\prod_{r:R} (r=0) \lor (r \neq 0)$, which is false.
      \end{proof}

      \begin{remark}
        This lemma cannot be improved in the sense that here is no finitely adequate $R$-module $M$ that is not finitely presented.
        Indeed, if $M$ were not finitely presented, then it would be not finite free.
        But finitely adequate modules are \emph{not not} finite free.
        A similar statement holds for linearly adequate.
      \end{remark}

      \begin{lemma}
        \label{identification-hom-tensor-for-fp-algebras}
        Let $M$ be a reflexive and quasi-coherent $R$-module.
        Then for every finitely presented $R$-algebra $A$ the map
        \[
          M \otimes A \to \Hom_R(M^\ast, A), \;\;
          m \otimes a \mapsto (\lambda \mapsto \lambda(m)a)
        \]
        is an equivalence which is natural in $A$.
        In the hom-set we forget the algebra structure of $A$ and consider it as an $R$-module.
      \end{lemma}

      \begin{proof}
        Consider the following chain of equivalences:
        \begin{align*}
          M \otimes A &\to M^{\Spec A} \\
                      &\to (M^{\ast\ast})^{\Spec A} \\
                      &\to \Hom_R(M^\ast, R^{\Spec A}) \\
                      &\leftarrow \Hom_R(M^\ast, A)
        \end{align*}
        The first is due to quasi-coherence, the second to reflexivity and the last to duality.
        The penultimate equality follows by currying since $R^{\Spec A}$ carries the pointwise module structure.
        We omit the verification that this composition agrees with the stated map of the lemma.
      \end{proof}

      Next we show that an $R$-module is finitely adequate and quasi-coherent if and only if it is finitely presented.

      \begin{corollary}
        \label{identification-hom-tensor-for-fp-modules}
        Let $M$ be a reflexive and quasi-coherent $R$-module.
        Then for every finitely presented $R$-module $N$ the map
        \[
          M \otimes N \to \Hom_R(M^\ast, N), \;\;
          m \otimes n \mapsto (\lambda \mapsto \lambda(m)n)
        \]
        is an equivalence which is natural in $N$.
      \end{corollary}

      {\color{red} The following could (probably should) be put into more elegant terms by using $\mathbb D(N) = \Spec (R \oplus N) = N_1(N^\ast)$ and explaing how it maps SES to fiber sequences.}

      \begin{proof}
        Consider the trivial square-zero extension $R \oplus N$ of $N$, which is a finitely presented $R$-algebra and fits into an exact sequence
        \[
          0 \to N \to R \oplus N \overset{\pi}{\to} R \to 0\rlap{.}
        \]
        This sequence is split exact as a sequence of $R$-modules, hence any additive functor will preserve its exactness.
        The previous lemma now gives a commutative diagram
        \[
        \begin{tikzcd}
          0 \ar[d] & 0 \ar[d] \\
          M \otimes N \ar[d]\ar[r, "(1)"] & \Hom_R(M^\ast,N) \ar[d] \\
          M\otimes (R\oplus N) \ar[r,"(2)"] \ar[d,"M \otimes \pi"'] & \operatorname{Hom}_R(M^\ast,R\oplus N) \ar[d,"\pi_\ast"] \\
          M \otimes R \ar[d]\ar[r,"(3)"] & \operatorname{Hom}_R(M^\ast,R) \ar[d] \\
          0 & 0
        \end{tikzcd}
      \]
      with exact columns.
      The map $(2)$ is an equivalence by \Cref{identification-hom-tensor-for-fp-algebras} and map $(3)$ is one by the reflexivity of $M$.
      By the $5$-lemma we see that $(1)$ is an equivalence as well.
      \end{proof}

      \begin{remark}
        Beware that the equivalence of \Cref{identification-hom-tensor-for-fp-modules} does not generalize to all finitely adequate $R$-modules $N$, even if we specialize to a finitely presented $M$.
        Indeed, this would imply that $M \otimes (-)$ and $\Hom_R(M^\ast,-)$ are exact functors.
        For $\Hom_R(M^\ast,-)$ this is fine, as in this case $M^\ast$ is a projective module, but for $M \otimes (-)$ this cannot be:

        Consider $M \equiv R/(r)$ and the monomorphism $(r) \lhook\joinrel\to R$.
        It induces the zero map $R/(r) \otimes (r) \to R/(r) \otimes R$, but it not the case that for all $r:R$ we have $R/(r) \otimes (r) = (r)/(r^2) = 0$, as this would imply the mere existence of an $a:R$ such that $r=ar^2$.
        Since $R$ is local we have $(ar \neq 0) \lor (1-ar \neq 0)$ and a simple case analysis would give $(r=0) \lor (r \neq 0)$ for all $r:R$.
      \end{remark}

      \begin{proposition}
        Let $M$ be an $R$-module that is finitely adequate and quasi-coherent.
        Then $\Hom_R(M^\ast,-)$ is exact when restricted to the category of finitely adequate $R$-modules.
        In particular, $M$ is finitely presented.
      \end{proposition}

      {\color{red} I also have a proof of this fact using the universal property of finitely adequate modules as a free abelian category.
        I haven't yet fully retraced Adelman's argument for this, so I will keep this (ugly) version of the proof for now.
      The relevant fact for other proof is that if an additive functor $F: \Mod R_{f.aq.} \to \Mod R$ is exact on fp modules and left exact on all f.aq. modules, then it must be naturally isomorphic to the exact continuation $K$ of $R^n \mapsto F(R^n)$.}

      \begin{proof}
        The second claim follows from the first together with the fact that the finitely presented modules are exactly the duals of the projectives in the category of finitely adequate modules.
        Thus it remains to show the first part.
        Suppose that $0 \to A \to B \to C \to 0$ is an exact sequence of finitely adequate modules.
        Choose finitely presented resolution $0 \to A \to P^0 \to P^1 \to P^2 \to 0$ and $0 \to C \to Q^0 \to Q^1 \to Q^2 \to 0$.
        Using the injectivity of $P_i$, we obtain a diagram
        \[
        \begin{tikzcd}
          & 0 \ar[d]
          & 0 \ar[d]
          & 0 \ar[d]
          \\
          0 \ar[r]
          & A \ar[r] \ar[d]
          & B \ar[r] \ar[d]
          & C \ar[r] \ar[d]
          & 0
          \\
          0 \ar[r]
          & P^0 \ar[r] \ar[d]
          & P^0 \oplus Q^0 \ar[r] \ar[d]
          & Q^0 \ar[r] \ar[d]
          & 0
          \\
          0 \ar[r]
          & P^1 \ar[r] \ar[d]
          & P^1 \oplus Q^1 \ar[r] \ar[d]
          & Q^1 \ar[r] \ar[d]
          & 0
          \\
          0 \ar[r]
          & P^2 \ar[r] \ar[d]
          & P^2 \oplus Q^2 \ar[r] \ar[d]
          & Q^2 \ar[r] \ar[d]
          & 0
          \\
          & 0
          & 0
          & 0
        \end{tikzcd}
        \]
        Applying $H :\equiv \Hom_R(M^\ast,-)$ preserves the exactness of the vertical sequences because of \Cref{identification-hom-tensor-for-fp-modules} together with the left exactness of $H$ and also preserves the exactness of split exact sequences, hence we get a diagram
        \[
        \begin{tikzcd}
          & 0 \ar[d]
          & 0 \ar[d]
          & 0 \ar[d]
          \\
          0 \ar[r]
          & H(A) \ar[r] \ar[d]
          & H(B) \ar[r] \ar[d]
          & H(C) \ar[r, "?", dashed] \ar[d]
          & 0
          \\
          0 \ar[r]
          & H(P^0) \ar[r] \ar[d]
          & H(P^0 \oplus Q^0) \ar[r] \ar[d]
          & H(Q^0) \ar[r] \ar[d]
          & 0
          \\
          0 \ar[r]
          & H(P^1) \ar[r] \ar[d]
          & H(P^1 \oplus Q^1) \ar[r] \ar[d]
          & H(Q^1) \ar[r] \ar[d]
          & 0
          \\
          0 \ar[r]
          & H(P^2) \ar[r] \ar[d]
          & H(P^2 \oplus Q^2) \ar[r] \ar[d]
          & H(Q^2) \ar[r] \ar[d]
          & 0
          \\
          & 0
          & 0
          & 0
        \end{tikzcd}
        \]
        The snake lemma gives an exact sequence $H(B) \to H(C) \to H(P^2) \to H(P^2 \oplus Q^2)$.
        Since $H(P^2) \to H(P^2 \oplus Q^2)$ is injective, the map $H(B) \to H(C)$ is surjective.
      \end{proof}
\end{enumerate}
